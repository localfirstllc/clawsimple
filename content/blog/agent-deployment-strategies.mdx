As soon as you start using AI as a "digital employee" for yourself or your small team, you'll quickly run into this problem: **"Can I have this AI act as a rigorous professional assistant in the work group, while being a casual, funny chatbot in the family group?"**

At first, most people fall into the "persona disaster zone": the AI was just helping you summarize an obscure financial report one second, and the next second it's replying to your mom’s recipe question with highly technical jargon. The scene can get quite awkward.

To avoid this AI "split personality," many think of creating several task-specific groups for discussions. But how should the underlying deployment actually handle this? In internet tech circles (like Discord and AutoGen/AI Agent communities), three main approaches have emerged.

Don't let the technical terms scare you—let's break these options down into plain English:

### Approach 1: One Nanny Running Through All Rooms (1 Agent in Multiple Groups)

The easiest and most direct method: we hire one digital nanny (a single, core AI bot) and add it to your work group, family group, and reading club simultaneously. By relying on the natural topic separation of platform group chats (every group is a different room), the topics are inherently kept apart.

> **Pro Tip:** If you add your Telegram Bot to a group chat and it doesn't respond, you may need to disable its Group Privacy mode via BotFather. Read [How to Enable Your Bot in Telegram Group Chats](/blog/how-to-enable-group-chat) for details.

**Who is it for?**
Independent users with simple needs. If what you need the nanny to do across all groups is almost exactly the same (e.g., just chatting, basic QA) and you don’t need to finely differentiate its roles, this is the fastest and easiest setup.

**What's the catch?**
The biggest issue is that **it’s still the exact same nanny with a locked personality.** If you give it "high-level permissions" (like sandbox tool execution) to delete or modify data files in the work group, it will carry those "dangerous scissors" into the family group too—a huge security risk. Moreover, if the same account talks too frequently across all active groups, platforms like Telegram or WhatsApp might mistake it for a spam bot and ban it permanently.

### Approach 2: Multiple Specialist Nannies in One House (1 Server, Multiple Agents)

This is currently the **most highly recommended and cost-effective approach** within the community.

Since a jack-of-all-trades nanny is prone to "splitting," why not hire several? One translation assistant, one coding engineer, and one dedicated customer service rep, ensuring each has its completely independent personality and workspace.

The beauty of this is that by utilizing **Multi-Agent Routing** technology, **you can have all these AI nannies share the exact same house (run on the same underlying server)!**

**Who is it for?**
It’s perfect for individuals or teams with specific operational niches. You want your work and personal digital assistants to absolutely never cross paths, and you can give them granular authorization policies—for instance, the customer service AI can only read documents, while the coding AI is allowed to do sandbox testing. Because they share a host environment, you get a small army of super-employees without spending an extra penny on cloud server fees.

**What's the catch?**
It is, after all, a shared house. If late one night the coding AI suddenly runs at full capacity (resource contention), it will hog all the water and electricity, causing your translation AI’s responses to slow down too. Worse still, if the house's power or internet gets cut (a single-point server crash), all five nannies will drop off the grid at the exact same time.

### Approach 3: A Single-Family Villa for Each Nanny (Multiple Servers, 1 Agent Each)

The ultimate, premium approach is physical-level absolute isolation: buying a standalone cloud server instance for every single AI assistant focused on a specific theme. The work assistant lives in a server in New York, while the confidential data-crunching bot lives on a server in Paris.

**Who is it for?**
Commercial App development teams, ToB enterprises, or community owners with massive concurrent traffic. Sometimes it's even just because specific nannies require heavy hardware (like an expensive GPU server for video processing), whereas a copywriting nanny only needs a cheap, standard server.

**What's the catch?**
Obviously, number one: **it’s way too expensive.** As your business and AI headcount grow, the server bills alone are enough to make your heart skip a beat. Two: even if budget isn't an issue, managing a dozen high-level network servers in different locations, updating base systems, and patching hacker vulnerabilities... unless you have a pure DevOps background, a regular founder will likely be driven mad by those endless terminal pop-ups.
